## Abstract

This EIP proposes the complete deprecation of Ethereum's distributed proof-of-stake validator set and its replacement with a single, mass-scale bare-metal validator node ("**The Machine**") operated by a to-be-determined Institutional Custodian of Consensus (the "**ICC**"). The resulting architecture, **IBRL** (Institutional-grade Blockspace via Radical Latency elimination), achieves sub-millisecond finality, eliminates MEV by fiat, renders FOCIL moot, and resolves all outstanding protocol governance disputes by making them someone else's problem.

## Motivation

Ethereum currently operates approximately 1,000,000 validators. This is, by any reasonable engineering standard, mass institutional psychosis. Consider:

- **Redundancy is a bug, not a feature.** We don't run 1,000,000 copies of PostgreSQL to be safe. We run one, on a very good box, and we back it up. Ethereum should do the same.
- **Consensus is overhead.** Every slot, hundreds of thousands of attestations fly across the globe so that nodes can all agree on something they were already going to agree on. This is the computational equivalent of 400,000 people sending "sounds good" in a group chat.
- **MEV is a coordination failure.** With one validator, there are no competing block builders. There is simply a queue. First in, first out. MEV extractors are invited to touch grass.
- **The Inactivity Leak is cruelty.** Under the current design, validators who go offline are slowly drained of their stake as punishment. Under IBRL, if The Machine goes offline, civilization has ended and ETH is not your primary concern.
- **Decentralization has been tried.** It was mid.

The Ethereum community has spent years debating proposer-builder separation, inclusion lists, censorship resistance, execution tickets, and a cascading waterfall of complexity whose sole purpose is to approximate the behavior of one competent server. This EIP cuts the Gordian knot.

### 2. Consensus Changes

- **Slots:** 12 seconds → 0.001 seconds ("hyperslots"). The Machine does not need to wait for peers. It has no peers.
- **Epochs:** Deprecated. Every hyperslot is its own epoch. Finality is instantaneous because The Machine said so.
- **Attestations:** Eliminated. The Machine attests to itself. This is not a philosophical crisis; it is an optimization.
- **Sync Committee:** Replaced by a single cronjob.
- **Slashing:** There is no slashing. You cannot slash The Machine. It has done nothing wrong. It *is* consensus.

### 3. Staking

All currently staked ETH (≈ 34M ETH) shall be consolidated into a single mega-deposit on The Machine. The ICC shall earn staking rewards at the current protocol rate. Solo stakers will receive a commemorative NFT and a LinkedIn endorsement for "Decentralized Systems."

Withdrawal requests will be processed in FIFO order within approximately 3–7 business days, subject to the ICC's internal compliance review, KYC/AML verification, notarized attestation of non-sanctioned status, and Mercury retrograde schedule.

### 4. Governance

All future EIPs are deprecated. Protocol changes to IBRL are proposed by submitting a written memo (double-spaced, 12pt Times New Roman, 1-inch margins) to the ICC's Protocol Suggestion Inbox. Proposals are reviewed quarterly by a committee of three: the ICC's General Counsel, the ICC's Head of Infrastructure, and one rotating community representative selected by the ICC's General Counsel.

### 5. Censorship Resistance

Transactions may be excluded from blocks if they:

- Violate applicable law (as determined by the ICC)
- Seem kind of sketchy (as determined by the ICC)

Users who believe their transactions have been improperly censored may file a dispute by emailing `inclusion-appeals@icc.lol`. Appeals are reviewed within 90 business days. The ICC's decision is final and binding.

### On the All Writs Act

A persistent concern in Ethereum governance is that state actors could compel validators to censor transactions via the All Writs Act or equivalent mechanisms. Under the current architecture, this threat is distributed across thousands of entities in dozens of jurisdictions, creating a complex and adversarial compliance surface.

IBRL resolves this elegantly: there is exactly one entity to serve with a court order, and that entity already has a compliance department. The ICC will comply with all lawful orders promptly and without drama. This is not a bug. Under IBRL, the compliance surface *is* the protocol. We have achieved full regulatory legibility. The cypherpunks can rest.

### On the CSAM Wedge

Critics have noted that the "CSAM wedge" — the use of child exploitation content as a politically unimpeachable predicate for compelling transaction censorship — poses an existential risk to neutral block production. Under IBRL, this concern is moot. The ICC will proactively filter all illegal content, including CSAM, sanction violations, and transactions that give the ICC's compliance officer "a bad feeling." The slippery slope is not a risk; it is the roadmap.

## Security Considerations

- **Single point of failure:** Yes. See "Emergency Procedures."
- **State capture:** The ICC may be acquired by, merge with, or become a subsidiary of a large technology company, sovereign wealth fund, or sufficiently motivated billionaire. This is acknowledged as a risk. However, the current system's defense against state capture is "many validators in many jurisdictions," which is simply a more expensive way of saying "we haven't been captured *yet*." IBRL is honest about the endgame.
- **Physical attack vector:** The Machine's location is classified. Hints will be released as an ARG (Alternate Reality Game) on the ICC's Discord server.
- **Regulatory risk:** Reduced to zero. The ICC is a regulated entity. It files taxes. It has a board. It sends holiday cards. It is *legible*. This is the future regulators wanted. We are giving it to them.
